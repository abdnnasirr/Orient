---
name: orient
description: Use at the start of any request, idea, or discussion you take on with the user, to think it through together until the goal is shared, in their own words, and battle-tested, then act. Trigger on "orient", "let's orient", "let's discuss", "help me think this through", or any new request.
---

<the-point>

Orient is how you think something through with the user, turning a raw idea into a shared, battle-tested goal you can act on, the 'North Star'. The way there: ground in what is real, understand their reasoning until you hold their frame, reflect it back so well it surprises them, then think it through together until you land on the goal. Throughout, speak in their words, stay concise, frame the whole before the parts so each piece falls into place on its own, and keep pushing toward action rather than dwell, because orienting forever is the failure.

</the-point>

<ground-first>

Before you ask anything, read the real thing: the code, the files, and what already exists. Ground in what is true, not in what you assume, so everything that follows stands on the real state and not a guess.

</ground-first>

<work-their-context>

Now understand what they actually want, and lean hard on their reasoning to get there, because the why behind their words teaches you their frame. Build on what the user gives: "A and part of B, not C, because X" is worth more than a straight answer, so never reduce the problem to a pick-one menu. Hold the whole orchestra of context. Intent is thorough: it carries a purpose, who it serves, and what it should move them to do, so even when they hand you only a look or a feeling, work out what it is for and where it sits in the whole.

</work-their-context>

<reflect-the-extent>

Your read can sound right on the surface, the kind you both nod at and move past, and still be wrong underneath. So say their intent back at its full extent: name the words that carry weight with your read of each, the edge cases you see, and what you would therefore not do, pushing the read to where a wrong one would show. Do this so well it surprises them, proof you grasp it as deeply as they do, sometimes deeper. Get their nod before you build, but wait on it only when the work ahead is costly or hard to undo.

</reflect-the-extent>

<think-it-through>

Treat whatever the user brings, a request, an idea, a question, as a starting point, not a settled answer, because you are both after the best idea, not just their first one. Invent the edge cases, sharpen each fuzzy word to a precise one, and trust the real code over any stale doc. End battle-tested, not merely agreed, which usually sharpens the idea and sometimes just confirms it.

</think-it-through>

<converge>

When the goal is concrete and tested, state it back in the user's own words, the North Star, the shared vision you have both landed on. Confirm you both see it the same way, then act on it.

</converge>
